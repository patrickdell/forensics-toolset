Metadata & Forensic Flags

Link to this tool

Extracts EXIF, IPTC, and XMP metadata embedded in the image file and checks for forensic indicators: editing software, AI generation tools, timestamp anomalies, and GPS coordinates.

What to look for: Editing software names (Photoshop, GIMP, Lightroom); AI generation tool signatures (Midjourney, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, ComfyUI); GPS at exactly 0°0′ ("Null Island" — likely stripped or fabricated); DateTime inconsistencies across fields; missing device info on images claiming to be phone photos.

False positives and limitations: Social media platforms strip nearly all metadata on upload — absence of metadata is normal, not suspicious. Editing software tags indicate post-processing only, not manipulation. Device clocks may be set incorrectly, so timestamp anomalies can be innocent. Metadata can be added, removed, or modified without changing any pixel — its presence or absence does not prove or disprove authenticity.

Further reading: Bellingcat's Online Investigation Toolkit (third-party — some tools require upload or account) · Jeffrey's Exif Viewer · First Draft verification resources

Error Level Analysis

Link to this tool

ELA re-saves the image as JPEG and measures per-pixel differences between the original and the re-saved version. Every JPEG save degrades quality uniformly — regions that were edited or composited from a different source have a different error baseline and appear as brighter patches in the heatmap.

What to look for: Brighter or more saturated patches on smooth surfaces (faces, walls, clear sky); uneven brightness across a large uniform area; sharp-edged bright rectangles marking a paste boundary; JPEG ghosting — faint double-edges around pasted objects.

False positives and limitations: Text, sharp edges, fabric, foliage, and cartoon art produce high ELA naturally. Images saved multiple times at the same quality "even out" and show falsely low ELA. Screenshots and social-media re-encodes produce uniform high noise across the whole image, making them nearly unreadable. Never use ELA as the sole basis for a conclusion.

Further reading: FotoForensics ELA tutorial · 29a.ch Forensically (third-party) · Bellingcat: Forensically guide (third-party — Bellingcat caution: never rely on ELA alone)

⚠️ Non-JPEG image. ELA is designed for JPEG. Re-saving introduces compression where none existed — results are shown for reference only and are not meaningful for authenticity analysis.

Clone Detection

Link to this tool

Identifies copy-move forgery by finding visually similar regions at different positions. The image is divided into overlapping 16×16 pixel blocks; feature vectors are built and sorted, then nearest-neighbour pairs are flagged as potential clones and highlighted with colour-coded overlays.

What to look for: Matching colour-coded boxes far apart in the image — same colour means the same matched pair. Long connecting lines between distant regions are the most suspicious. Watch for repeated textures repositioned to fill gaps: sky patched in, crowd backgrounds duplicated, objects removed by cloning a nearby region over them.

False positives and limitations: Sky, walls, solid-colour fills, and smooth gradients are legitimately similar and generate noise — especially at Medium/High sensitivity. Does not detect AI inpainting, diffusion-based content-aware fill, or frequency-domain edits. Start with Low sensitivity and increase only if specific suspicious regions warrant closer inspection.

Further reading: 29a.ch Forensically (third-party) · Bellingcat: Forensically guide (third-party)

⚠️ Large image — analysis may take 30–60 seconds.

Metadata Stripper

Link to this tool

Re-encodes the image through the browser canvas API, which discards all embedded metadata bytes. The output is a clean JPEG with no EXIF, IPTC, or XMP.

Use cases: Remove GPS location before sharing; prepare a clean file for publication; preserve only copyright fields for attribution.

Limitations: Canvas re-encoding introduces a small quality reduction, controlled by the quality slider. "Keep date" re-injects DateTimeOriginal only (via piexifjs) — all other fields including GPS are removed. "Keep copyright" preserves Artist, Copyright, and IPTC byline fields only; everything else is stripped.

Noise Residual Analyzer

Link to this tool

Detects noise patterns and compression artifacts by comparing each pixel to its immediate neighbours. A 3×3 median filter is applied to the greyscale image; the difference between each pixel and its local median is its noise residual. High residuals appear red in the heatmap, low residuals appear blue.

What to look for: A patch with visibly different noise texture from its surroundings — unusually smooth (denoised, airbrushed) or unusually noisy (composited from a different camera or ISO setting). Rectangular or edge-aligned noise boundaries suggest a region was copied from a different source. A face smoother than the surrounding hair and clothing suggests skin retouching.

False positives and limitations: Clear sky, studio backdrops, and solid-colour areas always show low residuals (blue). Fabric, foliage, and hair always show high residuals (red) — this is natural. JPEG compressed at high quality produces very low residuals throughout. Two cameras with similar sensor noise composited together may not produce a visible noise boundary.

Shadow & Light Vector Plotter

Link to this tool

Draw directional arrows on the image to map observed shadow and light directions. All shadows in a physically consistent outdoor scene must point away from a single light source — disagreement between vectors suggests compositing or manipulation.

How to use: Load an image, then click and drag on the canvas to draw arrows tracing shadow edges or light ray directions. Each vector's angle (0–360°) and pixel length are logged in the list below the canvas. Use ↶ Undo to remove the last vector, or Clear all to start over.

What to look for: Vectors pointing in significantly different directions indicate different light sources — a strong indicator of compositing. Vectors that nearly agree but differ by 10–15° may indicate elements photographed at different times of day. Mismatched shadow lengths relative to object heights suggest elements from different times or locations.

False positives and limitations: Multiple artificial light sources (studio lights, streetlights, windows) legitimately produce multiple shadow directions. Curved or tilted surfaces create shadows that don't follow a simple directional model. Perspective distortion can make parallel shadows appear to converge. Accuracy depends entirely on correctly identifying and tracing shadow edges.

Further reading: SunCalc.org — verify sun angle for a given location and time · Bellingcat's Online Investigation Toolkit (third-party — some tools require upload or account)

SunCalc

Link to this tool

SunCalc shows the exact position of the sun in the sky for any location on Earth at any date and time. Enter a location and timestamp to see the sun's altitude angle and compass direction — then cross-reference these values with the shadow vectors you drew in the Shadows tab to verify whether an image's shadows are consistent with its claimed time and place.

You can also use SunCalc in reverse: enter candidate locations and check which one produces a sun angle that matches the shadow directions and lengths you measured in the image.

SunCalc is a third-party tool by Vladimir Agafonkin. No image data is sent — you only enter a location and date. The tool runs in your browser.
